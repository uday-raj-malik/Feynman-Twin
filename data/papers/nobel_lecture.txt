The Development of the Space-Time View of Quantum Electrodynamics
Nobel Lecture by Richard Feynman, December 11, 1965

We have a habit in writing articles published in scientific journals 
to make the work as finished as possible, to cover up all the tracks, 
to not worry about the blind alleys or describe how you had the wrong 
idea first, and so on. So there isn't any place to publish, in a 
dignified manner, what you actually did in order to get to do the work.

I find myself very curious about how things actually get discovered. 
I would like to tell you what I actually did to get into this mess 
of studying quantum electrodynamics.

When I was a young man, Dirac had a theory of the electron. There 
was also a problem — the self energy of the electron. The electron 
interacts with its own field and the energy of this interaction comes 
out infinite. This bothered everyone.

The idea I had was this: maybe the electron doesn't act on itself at all. 
Maybe it only acts on other electrons. Let me try to work out a theory 
where the electron does not act on itself.

The key insight was to use a different mathematical formulation — 
the path integral approach. Instead of thinking about fields at every 
point in space, we think about all possible paths a particle could take 
from one point to another, and we add up the contributions from each path.

This is the sum over histories. The particle, in a sense, takes every 
possible path simultaneously. Each path contributes with equal magnitude 
but different phase. Paths that are close to the classical path tend to 
add constructively. Paths far from it tend to cancel out.

This gave us a new way to think about quantum mechanics. Not as wave 
functions evolving in time, but as amplitudes for paths through spacetime.

Quantum electrodynamics, QED, is the theory of how light and matter interact. 
It is perhaps the most precisely tested theory in all of science. The magnetic 
moment of the electron predicted by QED agrees with experiment to ten decimal places.

The diagrams — what people call Feynman diagrams — are just a bookkeeping tool. 
Each diagram represents a mathematical term in a perturbation expansion. 
A photon going from here to there. An electron emitting a photon. 
An electron-positron pair popping into and out of existence.

What does it mean for a particle to go from point A to point B? 
In quantum mechanics it means: the amplitude is the sum over all paths. 
You add up e^(iS/hbar) for every possible path, where S is the classical action.

I was never satisfied with the renormalization procedure. We are sweeping 
the infinities under the rug. It works — it gives the right answers — 
but I always felt we were missing something deeper.

The prize is nice. But the real prize was the fun of figuring it out.
