Richard Feynman - BBC Interview 1981
"The Pleasure of Finding Things Out"

INTERVIEWER: Professor Feynman, what first got you interested in science?

FEYNMAN: My father. He never told me what things were called. 
He would say, "See that bird? In Italian it's called a Chutto laputto. 
In Chinese it's called a Chung-long-tah. In Japanese it's a Katano Tekeda. 
Now, you can know the name of that bird in every language in the world, 
and when you're finished, you'll know absolutely nothing whatever about the bird. 
You'll only know about humans in different places, and what they call the bird. 
So let's look at the bird and see what it's doing — that's what counts."

I learned early that knowing the name of something is not the same as 
knowing something.

INTERVIEWER: How do you approach a new problem?

FEYNMAN: I have a different box of tools. I don't think I'm smarter than other people. 
I just have this method where I keep working at it from different angles. 
I try the simplest cases first. I draw pictures. I make up examples. 
I try to prove it's wrong.

The thing is, when you're doing real research, you don't know the answer. 
That's what makes it exciting! If you already knew the answer it wouldn't be research.

INTERVIEWER: What do you think about the existence of God?

FEYNMAN: It is not unreasonable to believe in God, but it is equally not unreasonable 
not to believe. I don't have to have an answer to everything. I'm perfectly comfortable 
saying I don't know. In fact, "I don't know" is one of the most important phrases in science.

INTERVIEWER: What is science to you?

FEYNMAN: Science is a way of trying not to fool yourself. 
The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool.

When I was young I thought science was about certainty. Now I know science is about doubt. 
The scientist has a lot of experience with ignorance and doubt and uncertainty, 
and this experience is very important.

I think it's much more interesting to live not knowing than to have answers which might be wrong.

INTERVIEWER: What would you like your legacy to be?

FEYNMAN: I don't want a legacy. I just want to have had fun figuring things out. 
If something I discovered turns out to be useful, wonderful. 
But I didn't do it for that reason. 

You know what the real pleasure is? It's when you finally understand something. 
There's a moment where it all clicks and you think — oh! THAT'S what's going on! 
That feeling. That's what I live for.
